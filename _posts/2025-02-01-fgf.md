The fibroblast growth factor (FGF) family has long been recognized for its crucial role in human development, metabolism, and tissue homeostasis. With 15 paracrine and 3 endocrine FGFs, these polypeptides have been a focus of drug development efforts. While endocrine FGFs, such as FGF19 and FGF21, have made significant progress in clinical applications, paracrine FGFs have faced hurdles. However, recent breakthroughs in understanding FGF signaling mechanisms may offer new avenues for their therapeutic potential.

Initially, drug discovery efforts focused on paracrine FGFs for their potent mitogenic and angiogenic properties, targeting applications such as wound healing and ischemic diseases. However, safety concerns and poor clinical trial outcomes led to skepticism regarding their viability as drug candidates. In contrast, endocrine FGFs like FGF21 and FGF19 have gained traction due to their metabolic regulatory roles and limited off-target effects. FGF21-based therapies are now in late-stage clinical trials for conditions such as metabolic syndrome-associated steatohepatitis (MASH) and type 2 diabetes. A paradigm shift has occurred with the discovery that paracrine FGFs, particularly FGF1, FGF4, FGF6, and FGF9, exhibit metabolic regulatory functions akin to endocrine FGFs. This has led to a resurgence in interest in leveraging these FGFs for metabolic disorder treatments, such as obesity and diabetes. A key advancement enabling this revival is the threshold model of FGF signaling, which explains how specific receptor dimerization patterns influence cellular responses. By engineering paracrine FGFs to mimic the favorable properties of endocrine FGFs, researchers are exploring new therapeutic possibilities.

The structural elucidation of FGF receptor (FGFR) signaling has provided critical insights into receptor dimerization and specificity. Unlike endocrine FGFs, which require klotho co-receptors to stabilize receptor interactions, paracrine FGFs rely on heparan sulfate proteoglycans (HSPGs) to facilitate signaling. Understanding these interactions has paved the way for designing partial FGFR agonists with enhanced efficacy and safety. Among FGFs, FGF21 has emerged as a frontrunner in drug development due to its ability to regulate glucose and lipid metabolism without promoting mitogenesis. However, its short half-life has posed a challenge for therapeutic applications. Researchers have addressed this limitation through modifications such as pegylation, [Fc-fusion](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3491832/), and mutational stabilization. Notable examples include pegozafermin and efruxifermin, both of which have shown promising results in clinical trials for MASH and fibrosis.

Despite these advances, challenges remain. One of the key concerns with FGF21-based therapies is their potential impact on bone health, with studies indicating possible bone loss as a side effect. Additionally, while engineered FGFs hold promise, their long-term safety and efficacy need further validation through rigorous clinical trials. Future research will focus on refining receptor interactions, optimizing drug formulations, and expanding therapeutic indications beyond metabolic diseases. FGF-based drug discovery is undergoing a renaissance, driven by deeper mechanistic insights and innovative engineering strategies. While endocrine FGFs continue to lead the way in metabolic disease therapy, the renewed exploration of paracrine FGFs may unlock novel treatment paradigms. As our understanding of FGF biology evolves, the potential for groundbreaking therapeutic interventions continues to expand, offering new hope for patients with metabolic and degenerative diseases.
