Artificial Intelligence (AI) has continued to evolve, with each generation of models bringing new innovations that redefine what’s possible. **DeepSeek R1** is a remarkable entry into this landscape, setting itself apart from traditional models like GPT-4 with its focus on reasoning capabilities, efficiency, and reinforcement learning (RL)-centric design. In this post, we’ll take a deep dive into what makes DeepSeek R1 unique and the implications it holds for the AI hardware and software ecosystem.

## Reinventing Reasoning with RL-Centric Design
DeepSeek R1’s standout feature is its reliance on **Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)**, a reinforcement learning methodology that bypasses traditional supervised fine-tuning (SFT). While most large language models (LLMs) depend heavily on SFT to refine capabilities, DeepSeek R1 demonstrates that advanced reasoning capabilities can emerge directly through RL. 

_GRPO_ extends classical _PPO_ by incorporating **relative rewards** into the training process. In standard RL, policies are updated based on absolute rewards. GRPO instead compares group-level performance metrics to ensure improvements are made relative to other samples within the training batch. This relative metric allows the system to focus on incremental improvements within specific contexts (e.g., logical reasoning in Chain of Thought (CoT) tasks). GRPO operates at _group granularity_ where performance is assessed collectively rather than on an individual sample level. For instance CoT samples with similar prompts or outputs are grouped, and their cumulative performance determines reward distribution. 

GRPO uses careful designed reward signals to encourage desired behaviours such as _logical coherence_ and _long chain reasoning_ (positive reinforcement for well-structured, step by step answer), and negative rewards penalize incoherence or contradictions in the reasoning process. Similar to **PPO**, **GRPO** applies policy clipping to prevent overly large updates (which can destabilize training), and ensures smooth policy transitions/maintains training stability across iterations. This enables the model to learn complex, multi-step reasoning without explicit labeled examples; by focusing on relative improvements, GRPO optimizes the model incrementally, reducing computational overhead compared to training purely on absolute metrics.

Goal-conditioned RL enables personalized decision-making by integrating patient-specific anatomical data into the model.

**Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** 

Supervised Fine Tuning is a traditional training method used in LLMs like GPT-4, where a pretrained model is fine-tuned on task-specific labeled datasets. SFT operates on a _pretrained model_ (e.g. GPT or transformer based architecture) and the based model is trained on a vast corpus of generic text data to learn language patterns and semantic relationships. It requires **large scale, labeled** datasets tailored to the specific task; for reasoning tasks, these datasets include input-output pairs where the correct answers or outputs are explicitly provided. 

The model's weights are updated through **gradient descent** using a task-specific loss function (e.g. cross entropy) and the objective is to minimize the difference between the predicted output and the labeled ground truth. This is imaginibly heavily dependent on high-quality labeled datasets (expensive), and training on massive labeled datasets requires significant computational resources. There is a risk of overfitting as well (loss of generality due to narrow datasets). **DeepSeek R1** combines the strengths of both methods (cold-start with SFT) early stages use curated datasets to boostrap CoT reasoning and ensure output readability, and later stages apply GRPO to refine and enhance reasoning capabilities focusing on long-term task generalization. 

For our SMI, we can apply GRPO that dynamically adjusts inputs based on task-specific feedback. GRPO can refine and adapt the prompts fed into SAM based on real-time segmentation quality metrics. For example, during a robotic-assisted surgery, the system can iteratively learn to identify the most relevant regions of interest (ROIs) by optimizing the prompts that align with surgical goals such as identifying critical anatomy or potential complications. 

**PPO and GRPO** 

Recall that a _policy_ in RL is a strategy or a set of rules that tells an agent what action to take in a given situation (simply put, the "brain" of an agent, guiding its decisions to maximize reward over time). Mathematically, a policy 
$$\pi(a:s)$$ 
is a function mapping from a state $$s$$ (current situation) to an action $$a$$ (what the agent should do). Note that there are two "flavours" of policies: 

- **Deterministic Policies:** These always choose the same action for a given state e.g. "if the light is green, then drive forward" 
- **Stochastic Policies:** These choose actions with certain probabilities, for instance "if the light is orange, drive forward with a probability of 30%"

policies, define how agents interact with their environment -- and the goal of reinforcement learning is to _find an optimal policy_ the maximizes long-term rewards. Why should we care about this framework? Well, traditional AI (or rather, supervised learning) requires a-priori, labeled datasets that are input-output pairs (e.g. image/label) and trains the model to mimic the correct answer in the dataset (e.g. this image is a cat or a dog). The beauty of reinforcement leanring is that _we do not a priori require labeled data to learn_. This is the key. Instead the agent learns by interacting with the environment and receiving feedback (reward/penalty) and is useful when the desired output depends on a sequence of decisions, rather than a single input-output mapping. This is why traditionally situations like Chess, Go or game-playing have been susceptible to RL-based methods.

Hence RL shines brightest when outcomes are influenced by a series of actions - robotics, game playing, and when an explicit dataset is not available but feedback can still be given (e.g. it is hard to create a comprehensive, expansive labeled dataset for chess, but we can "learn" strategies). For instance, in drug discovery perhaps we don't really have true labeled for certain states and configurations, but we can explore the state space of our environment to discovery optimal behavior. 

The beauty of DeepSeek is that they embedded the RL-philosophy into LLMs, where each next token selection is defined by the learned policy; cleverly defining rewards for _logical coherence_ and penalties for _logical fallacies_ and contradictions. For instance, in a multi-step math problem, a sequence that arraives at the correct answer using valid intermediate steps gets a higher reward. Altering the reward for other desirable metrics (such as creativity, or flourish, or style) can adapt the model towards differnt tasks (for example writing Game of Thrones!). 

$$
L^{PPO}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \min\left(r_t(\theta) A_t, \text{clip}\left(r_t(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon\right) A_t\right) \right]

L^{GRPO}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_G \left[ \sum_{i \in G} \min\left(r_i(\theta) A_i, \text{clip}\left(r_i(\theta), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon\right) A_i\right) \right]
$$

Before we dive into DeepSeek's GRPO optimization, let's talk about vanilla PPO. PPO is a popular reinforcement learning algorithm in the family of policy gradient methods, it strikes a balance between policy improvement and training stability through constrained updates, making it well suited for many RL tasks. It is designed to balance _exploration_ and _exploitation_ by first preventing large destabilizing updates through limiting how much a new policy can diverge from an old one. It guides policy updates by comparing the action's reward to the average performance of other actions in the same state. GRPO on the other hand builds on PPO by optimizing relative performance across groups of samples, making it particularly well-suited for tasks that require _comparative reasoning_ (evaluates the performance of actions relative to others within a group of similar tasks or states e.g. comparing multiple reasoning chains in a language model for logical coherence), and _group level aggregation_ (instead of evaluating each sample independently, we aggregate performnace metrics across groups and use these collective metrics to update the policy). This makes GRPO markedly more resilient to noise and sparsity in the reward signal, and enables us to prioritize high level objectives (e.g. logical consistency) whilst fine tuning low level details such as token-level coherence. This is ideal for chain of thought reasoning (e.g. answering a multi-step math problem), multi-agent systems (surgical robotics where tools, cameras and multiple agents interact), and sparse/noisy environments. 

In DeepSeek, the group occurs based on task similarity with shared characteristics, for instance prompts that require multi-step reasoning may be grouped together, and single-step reasoning prompts are grouped together. Tasks with similar inputs contribute to grouping (questions with similar structures such as "12 + 34" or "56 - 23") are grouped, and potentially we group inputs by the cosine similarity of their embeddings (partition the embedding space into spatial regions). Tasks with similar output expectations may also influence grouping i.e. summarization tasks requiring concise, factual response form one group whilst creative writing tasks form another. Contextual features can also influence grouping, where easier/harder tasks may be grouped separately and similar knowledge areas may form groups/clusters. DeepSeek forms these groups **dynamically** in real time during training, adapting to evolving tasks/prompts and changing contextual environments. This however adds extra computational overhead: 

1. The model first computes embeddings for tasks/states using an internal representation, and features such as semantic similarity, difficulty or task structure are subsequently extracted
2. k-means and nearest neighbor cluster are used to form groups based on the extracted features, where the number of clusters/groups can either be predetermined or adaptively chosen base don task distribution
3. For each extracted group, the model calculates group-relative rewards by comparing the performance of actions or outputs across the group (for instance, groups of tasks focused on arithmetic would learn different policies to groups of tasks focused on geometry and angle chasing)

## Philosophical Reflections on what DeepSeek means

The emergence of DeepSeek R1 not only represents a technical milestone but also invites a deeper reflection on how intelligence is evolving in both machines and humanity. This model challenges conventional wisdom in AI by shifting from brute-force scaling to a focus on adaptability, efficiency, and reasoning, mirroring lessons from biology and evolution itself. At its core, DeepSeek redefines intelligence as a system’s ability to adapt dynamically to its environment, rather than relying on sheer computational power. This mirrors human intelligence, where adaptability, context-awareness, and the ability to learn from feedback often matter more than raw cognitive capability. In this way, DeepSeek prompts us to consider whether the next frontier for both AI and humanity lies not in building bigger systems but in optimizing the resources we already have.

DeepSeek’s reliance on iterative feedback through GRPO also emphasizes the importance of learning through failure and refinement. This iterative process, central to reinforcement learning, resonates with the human experience of trial and error, where growth is often driven by the lessons we glean from our mistakes. As AI evolves, it encourages us to embrace failure as an essential part of the journey toward mastery. Moreover, the efficiency-driven design of DeepSeek shifts focus from traditional paradigms of general-purpose models to systems designed for specialized tasks. This raises a philosophical question: Is the future of intelligence rooted in versatility or in targeted excellence? DeepSeek suggests the latter, emphasizing that intelligence can emerge from focusing on specific goals and refining solutions tailored to unique challenges.

DeepSeek's exploration-exploitation balance—the delicate act of deciding between refining known strategies or exploring new possibilities—reflects a dilemma humans face daily, whether in life decisions, innovation, or societal progress. Its success in maintaining this balance reminds us that progress is neither linear nor solely about pushing boundaries; it often requires knowing when to refine and when to venture into the unknown. Ultimately, DeepSeek is more than just an engineering achievement—it is a mirror reflecting the evolving nature of intelligence and our own aspirations. As we build increasingly sophisticated AI systems, it forces us to ask not just what machines can do, but what we, as humans, can learn from their design, processes, and goals.
