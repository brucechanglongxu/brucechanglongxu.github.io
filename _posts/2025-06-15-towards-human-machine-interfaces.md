Brain–computer interfaces (BCIs) are no longer a speculative fringe—they are becoming the next substrate of communication, control, and cognition. In the last decade, we have witnessed a transition from crude scalp EEGs to precision intracortical arrays, from experimental cursor control to FDA-cleared systems enabling communication in locked-in patients. But beyond these headlines, a deeper shift is unfolding: the convergence of minimally invasive recording, adaptive neuromodulation, and context-aware decoding is redefining what BCIs are for. They are no longer just reactive tools for restoration. Increasingly, they are proactive instruments for prevention.

Consider the frontier opened by Synchron, which pioneered a brain–computer interface that lives not in the cortex itself, but within the blood vessels that envelop it. Their Stentrode device is deployed via a catheter-based procedure into the superior sagittal sinus or adjacent cortical veins, placing 16 electrodes in intimate proximity to motor cortex without craniotomy. The electrodes record high-gamma band local field potentials (LFPs), typically between 70–200 Hz, which correlate strongly with population-level cortical spiking and are robust predictors of motor intention. Unlike traditional ECoG or penetrating arrays, the Stentrode achieves this without violating brain parenchyma, dramatically reducing the risk of gliosis and long-term signal degradation.

These signals, although lacking the granularity of single-unit activity (>300 Hz), offer a high signal-to-noise ratio for key BCI tasks. The system has demonstrated consistent performance in translating high-gamma amplitude modulations into click-based control schemes, enabling patients with ALS to operate smartphones, compose text, and interact with smart-home interfaces. Critically, Synchron's decoding pipeline relies on supervised learning algorithms trained on attempted movement paradigms, extracting spectral features from the gamma band and classifying motor intent with linear discriminant or support vector machines, depending on patient-specific characteristics.

This is not just an engineering marvel; it signals a sea change in BCI design philosophy. Instead of maximizing channel count and bit-rate at all costs, Synchron optimizes for safety, stability, and surgical scalability. With over 1,500 neuro-interventional cath labs worldwide capable of deploying their system, Synchron bypasses the bottlenecks of neurosurgical ORs, making high-fidelity BCIs feasible at scale. Early trial data from the COMMAND study report over 90% classification accuracy in binary selection tasks, with signal stability exceeding 12 months without notable decline.

Now imagine coupling this passive decoding with closed-loop neuromodulation: a future where the same interface that listens to your brain can speak back. This is the vision animating next-gen deep brain stimulation (DBS) systems—devices that no longer fire blindly, but respond to real-time biomarkers. Medtronic's Percept PC system, for instance, uses a sensing-enabled IPG to record beta-band activity from the subthalamic nucleus in Parkinson's disease, modulating stimulation only when pathological synchrony is detected. The NeuroPace RNS system applies a similar approach in epilepsy, detecting ictal bursts and delivering millisecond-latency stimulation to abort seizure spread. In psychiatric applications, researchers at UCSF and Emory are piloting adaptive mood DBS using gamma activity in the amygdala and cingulate cortex to forecast depressive relapses, intervening before subjective symptoms emerge.

The potential is staggering: a brain interface that not only restores lost function but anticipates decline, intervenes early, and reshapes the course of disease. It is medicine not as rescue, but as resonance. Technical advances in biomarker discovery, on-device ML inference, and ultra-low-power analog front ends are converging to enable these bidirectional interfaces. Devices with chronic sensing capability, such as Medtronic's BrainSense platform, are beginning to support embedded classifier inference, leveraging Cortex-M processors to run neural net architectures optimized for low-latency biomarker detection. The next frontier lies in integrating wearable telemetry—HRV, sleep state, galvanic skin response—to fuse physiological context with direct neural signals.

The future of BCIs, then, lies in this duality: decoding and modulation, listening and shaping, circuit-read and circuit-write. The questions now are no longer "Can we record?" or "Can we stimulate?" They are: What neural features are most predictive? How do we map intent onto action or state onto therapy? And perhaps most crucially, what does it mean to engineer with the brain, rather than merely on it?

We stand at the threshold of a new neurology—one where brain interfaces are not just prosthetics, but platforms. The time to build is now.
