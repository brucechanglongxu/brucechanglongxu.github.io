## Prokaryotic Gene Regulation (Operon Model)

Prokaryotic gene regulation is _efficient and rapid_, ensuring that bacteria only express genes when needed. The best models for understanding prokaryotic gene regulation are **operons**, which consist of:

1. **Promoter:** The site where RNA polymerase binds. 
2. **Operator:** The _"on/off switch"_ where repressors bind to block transcription. 
3. **Structural Genes:** The genes being transcribed and translated. 
4. **Regulatory Genes:** These encode repressors or activators that regulate the operon. 

Operons allow for coordinate expression of multiple genes under a single promoter, making gene expression efficient. 

**Lac Operon (Inducible System)** 

The lac operon controls the expression of lactose metabolism genes (lacZ, lacY, lacA) in E. coli. This operon is _inducible_, meaning that it is normally off, but turns on when lactose is present.

**Epigenetic Regulation** 

Epigenetic processes that stably alter gene expression patterns (and/or transmit the alterations at cell division) are thought to include 1. cytosine methylation 2. post-translational modifications of histone proteins and remodelling of chromatin 3. RNA-based mechanisms. Unlike the genome, which is essentially identical in all cells of a vertebrate and stable throughout the lifetime of an individual, the epigenome differs from cell to cell and is plastic, changing with time and with exposure to the environment. 

The establishment of stable patterns of gene expression is a pre-requisite of normal differentiation and is accomplished by the imposition The epigenome appears to be particularly vulnerable to environmental influences during certain stages of development (cleavage, perinatal period, puberty) and alterations in gene expression patterns induced at these times may eprsist for long periods, influencing the phenotype of the adult. The epigenome thus distinguishes one cell type from another and also appears to comprise the molecular memory that is inherited by daughter cells at mitosis. 

The epigenome thus encompasses a number of molecular componanets, with cytosine methylation, histone proteins, and their post-translational modification, chromatin remodeling complexes and various non-coding RNAs playign important roles. Most aspects of gene expression are influenced by epigenetic mechanisms, from relative accessibility of genes in the chromosomal landscape, through transcription and post-transscriptional RNA processing and stability to translation.
