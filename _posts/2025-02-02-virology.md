Viruses and bacteria are among the most enigmatic entities in biology -- microscopic agents that exist in the gray area between life and non-life. They lack cellular structures and the ability to reproduce independently, yet they have influenced evolution, shaped ecosystems, and driven medical advancements. The study of virology and bacteriology unveils both the destructive and transformative power of these microorganisms, providing key insights into genetics, immunity and biotechnology. _"There are not enough vaccines in the world."_ This reality underscores the ongoing battle against infectious diseases. Lower respiratory tract infections such as Pneumonia, Tuberculosis (TB), and Diarrheal diseases, claim millions of lives annually, particularly in developing nations where healthcare access is limited. Diarrheal diseases alone cause over two million deaths per year, often due to dehydration and gastrointestinal tract leakage. 

Tuberculosis is particularly concerning as it often co-infects individuals with HIV, further weakening the immune system. Moreover, many infectious diseases once thought to be eradicated have evolved resistance to therapeutic agents, leading to the emergence of _**escape pathogens**_ - microbes that have developed mechanisms to evade drugs and immune responses. To combat this, combination therapies, or **drug cocktails** are used in treatment strategies, such as those for **Helicobacter Pylori** which causes gastritis, ulcers and stomach cancer.
