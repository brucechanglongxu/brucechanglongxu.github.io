Metabolism is the fundamental process that powers life, converting food into energy, and maintaining cellular function. Understanding metabolism bridges biochemistry, physiology and endocrinology; The regulation of metabolism is largely controlled by hormones, which act as messengers, directing cells to store energy in times of abundance or mobilizing it during fasting. By mastering glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, glycogen metabolism and fatty acid breakdown, one can gain a holistic view of how the human body orchestrates energy balance. 

## Glycolysis and Gluconeogenesis: The balance between storage and production

At the heart of metabolism lies glycolysis, the breakdown of glucose to generate ATP. This cytoplasmic pathway provides rapid energy and is one of the few pathways that can funciton anaerobically, making it critical during intese exercise or low-oxygen conditions. Glycolysis begins when glucose enters the cell via GLUT transporters, is phorphorylated by hexokinase (or glucokinase in the liver), and undergoes a series of reactions leading to the formation of **pyruvate**. If oxygen is available, pyruvate enters the mitochondria for the Krebs cycle; if not, it is converted into lactate via fermentation to regenerate NAD+ for continued ATP production. 

On the other hand _gluconeogenesis_ ensures that glucose is available when dietary intake is sufficient. This occurs primarily in the **liver** and to a lesser extent, the **kidneys**. It is essentially the reverse of glycolysis, utilizing substrates such as lactate, glycerol, and amino acids to synthesize glucose. However, certain steps in glycolysis are **irreversible**, requiring alternative enzymes in gluconeogenesis. The conversion of **pyruvate back to glucose** is a highly regulated process, with hormones such as glucagon and cortisol promoting it, while insulin inhibits it. This interplay ensures that blood glucose levels remain stable, especially during fasting or prolonged exercise.

## Fatty Acid and Amino Acid Metabolism: Alternative Energy Pathways

When glucose is scarce, the body shifts to alternative fuel sources, primarily fatty acids and amino acids. Fatty acids undergo beta-oxidation, a mitochondrial process that breaks down long-chain fatty acids into **Acetyl-CoA**, feeding directly into the Krebs cycle.
