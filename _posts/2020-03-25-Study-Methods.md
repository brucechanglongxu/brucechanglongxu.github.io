I've recently had to completely reevalute my prior methods of studying - the fast-paced nature of going to school at Stanford has placed me in the uncomfortable position of having to grow into a more efficient and hopefully more effective student. I have compiled below a list of lessons that I've learnt during the past few months here that I hope to be of service to whomever is reading (including my future self):

Lesson 1: Time exploring is not wasted, but there is an optimal time to stop (Algorithms to live by) 

Gone are the days when I could lounge around for an hour choosing which problem I would be tackling for the next period or searching for thousands of textbooks that interest me and taking my time to choose between them. My experiences with the Math 60 series has taught me that although there are countless opportunities out there to learn, it is often the act of saying no and streamlining your studying methodologies that induce the greatest desired effect in the shortest period of time. Often what this means is picking one or two key resources, study buddies, or theorems I would like to use to start tackling a problem and begin execution immediately. Prepare well, but prepare enough: The optimal time to stop looking is when you cross that threshold of having enough resources and knowledge to be able to self-correct and self-improve, this is the sweet spot where subsequent learning to follow in a compounding effect. 

Lesson 2: Listen and learn from others but _focus on your own game_. 

Transitioning from the summer to the start of my freshman year absolutely shattered my sense of direction, identity and purpose. I'm grateful that I've had a support system that has slowly allowed me to gain traction on a new trajectory professionally, but I've also learned valuable lessons on how to process information in an age where you can literally find the answer to anything at the click of a button. On one hand you don't want to deprive yourself of the experiences and relationships that make the world worth living in - "know your surroundings and know yourself" - (Sun Tzu refactored), but on the other you don't want to be taking in a slew of competing opinions, world-views and life-advice that will pull north, east, south, west without any net direction. Self-doubt is inevitable: surround yourself with people who can both give it to you straight, who can offer an un-euphemised version of reality and people who lift you up and support dreams that are maybe a little high up. 

I recently played a card game with three of my housemates that broadly requires the collection of points using an interesting metric and there were two competing tactics you could follow to win: Completely obliterate the competition with (full - 0 - 0 - 0) i.e. "shoot for the moon" or having the _least_ points possible. I was lucky enough to accomplish the first on the first try, but I didn't really go in with the mentality of shooting for the moon, through a mixture of outside circumstances and the decisions I had made to get to that point, I simply found myself in the position with so many points where shooting for the moon was actually a more viable option to survive than playing the normal game. This is a lesson the value of serendipity and optimizing your decisions to surround whatever position you find yourself in. 

Lesson 3: More important that learning about when to say no - find the best time to say yes. 

This follows from the mentality of actively carving out the opportunities that matter to you or that you find are meaningful. There is always something more to do, or something more to achieve, it is just up to you to determine whether this is the _right time for *you*_ to be doing said activity, learning said material, engaging in said research project etc. Following the theme of lesson number 1, often you want to structure your activities so that they both diversify and compound on each other. 

Lesson 4: Professionally, it's the _substance_ that matters. 

There's no point in sending an email if it does serve a general purpose: Whether that'd be exploratory (e.g. shopping a class), of courteousy (e.g. introducing yourself), correcting a mistake, clarifying a question etc. Similarly, the problem sets that the professors give out always serve a purpose: don't underestimate them. There is virtually no point in 'collaboration' unless it is intentional, only go to office hours if you have a question you want to ask (which will always be true if you are taking the class correctly) and by the same vein, don't leave until you've gotten an answer to that question. You need to _be persistent_ and think about what will be best for you. Remember that it is the professors _job_ to be providing their students an education, and it is your absolute right to take full advantage of that. Even if the class is not the most effectively taught, you can still have a superior learning experience with the right methodologies. 

Lesson 5: Keep things in perspective.

What matters most? Family, being a good friend, being a good citizen, developing a broader view of the world and it's inequities. It's so easy to get caught up in the microdetails whilst losing sight of the bigger picture, remember, the ultimate goal of a neural network is the find the global solution rather than a local dip. This also applies on a more micro-level (e.g. essay-writing: what are the key themes of your essay, what are you actually trying to convey? Superfluous words and facts are nice, but your ultimate goal is build upon a compelling thesis). 

To conclude: Don't worry about where you are right now, the road ahead is long and what's most important is that you are failing and learning the key lessons early on to develop the life-habits that will hopefully propel you towards wherever you want to and need to go. 

P.S. I realised that I never really addressed the title: this entire blog post stemmed out of a few stray thoughts whilst I was trying to restructure the notes that I was taking on gene-mapping from a khan-academy video using proposition logic. My biggest takeaway from CS106B was that computer science was a discipline that centered on effective communication: writing readable, maintainable, tight code. My ground-breaking proposal is that given that we already have an entire discipline built around the topic, we should really be taking notes like we write code. Our first pass might be extremely messy as we are still learning the ropes, but streamilining our notes into something that mimics solid code (variables initialised first, eliminating redundancies, public and private class functions) really requries us to have a deeper understanding of the topic matter and our corresponding position of knowledge. I am by no means an expert on taking notes, but this was just something that crossed my mind. Stay-tuned for a Stanford Daily blog post along similar lines.
