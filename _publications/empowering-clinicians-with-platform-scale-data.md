excerpt: "This Perspective articulates a translational framework for using large-scale real-world data (RWD) platforms to inform clinical decision-making in scenarios where guidelines fall short. Modern electronic health records (EHRs) contain rich longitudinal data across millions of patients, but their complexity, fragmentation, and latency have long limited their bedside utility. This paper outlines how structured RWD—when harmonized and queried via cohort filters, embedding-based similarity search, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) interfaces—can support nuanced, individualized decisions in oncology, cardiology, emergency medicine, and rare disease.
By highlighting four recurring evidence gaps—(1) guideline equivalence zones, (2) personalization in common diseases, (3) prognosis estimation, and (4) rare or atypical disorders—the authors demonstrate how real-world platforms can extend clinical intuition beyond trial boundaries. The work further discusses technical and cultural barriers to adoption, including interoperability, clinician trust, and the need for transparent, auditable outputs. With illustrative case studies and proposed implementation strategies, the paper calls for embedding AI-powered, clinician-facing tools directly into workflows—turning retrospective data into real-time insight. It offers a vision of a future in which every patient encounter is informed not only by personal experience and published trials, but by a virtual cohort of analogous patients drawn from across the healthcare system."

Clinicians routinely face patient scenarios that lie outside guideline boundaries; randomized controlled trials cannot feasibly cover every phenotype, comorbidity constellation, or rare condition. This perspective proposes a translational framework that turns population-scale real-world data into point-of-care guidance and outlines the technical and cultural factors that will determine success. We synthesize advances in data aggregation and analytics, including cohort filtering, embedding-based similarity search, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). Examples from oncology, cardiology, emergency medicine, and rare disease illustrate how such tools can address evidence gaps and deliver nuanced clinical insights when trial data fall short.
