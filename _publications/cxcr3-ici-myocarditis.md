Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) are effective cancer therapies but may trigger myocarditis, a rare yet fatal immune-related adverse event. Using both genetic and pharmacological mouse models and single-cell multiomics, this study reveals that CXCL9/10+CCR2+ macrophages and CXCR3hi CD8+ T cells are central to disease pathogenesis. In vivo depletion of macrophages and CXCR3 blockade reduces myocardial inflammation, improves survival, and limits cardiomyocyte apoptosis. Patient biopsy data confirm translational relevance, highlighting the CXCR3-CXCL9/10 axis as a promising therapeutic target for ICI myocarditis and broader cardiac inflammation.
