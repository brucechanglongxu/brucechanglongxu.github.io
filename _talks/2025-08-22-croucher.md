Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have reshaped the landscape of oncology, but their efficacy comes with the cost of immune-related adverse events, including myocarditis—a condition with high mortality and limited therapeutic options. In our recent Circulation Research study, we identified the CXCR3–CXCL9/10 signaling axis as a key driver of immune-mediated cardiac inflammation. Using high-dimensional single-cell multiomics in both murine models and human biopsy samples, we revealed clonally expanded CXCR3⁺ cytotoxic T cells and inflammatory macrophages as central actors in disease pathogenesis. Blockade of CXCR3 significantly attenuated immune infiltration, cardiomyocyte apoptosis, and improved survival.

In this workshop, I will walk participants through the mechanistic underpinnings of ICI myocarditis and demonstrate how single-cell transcriptomic and TCR-based approaches can guide the development of targeted immunomodulatory therapies. Participants will gain insight into the interplay between T cells and macrophages in cardiac injury, and how this knowledge can translate into actionable strategies for intervention.

Toward the conclusion, I will offer a forward-looking perspective on AI-guided mRNA therapeutics — a paradigm that combines transformer-based protein and RNA sequence modeling with programmable delivery platforms. Leveraging breakthroughs in large-scale sequence modeling (e.g., ProGen2, ESM, mRNAid), we can now design synthetic mRNAs encoding therapeutic payloads — such as CXCR3 decoy receptors or immune-dampening cytokines — with tunable kinetics and cell-type specificity. When delivered via cardiotropic lipid nanoparticles, these mRNA therapies may offer temporally precise, tissue-targeted immune modulation, not only for myocarditis but for a broader class of inflammatory cardiovascular diseases.
